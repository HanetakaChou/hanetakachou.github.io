Diffusion Profile

Currently, all real time methods are based on the diffusion profile("14.4.2 Rendering with Diffusion Profile" of [dEon 2007]) rather than the BSSRDF.

The subsurface scattering is simulated by a much simpler process that the light of each location is scattered to the vicinal locations according to the weights indicated by the diffusion profile.

Evidently, an equivalent gather operation can be used to replace the scatter operation, and the diffuse term can be considered as "L⁡o(po)=∫AR⁡(po,pi)⋅BRDF⁡(pi,wi)⋅Li⁡(pi,wi)⋅∣cos⁡(θ)∣ dA\displaystyle \operatorname{L}_o(p_o) = \int_A \operatorname{R}(p_o, p_i) \cdot \operatorname{BRDF}(p_i, w_i) \cdot \operatorname{L_i}(p_i, w_i) \cdot |\cos(\theta)| \, dALo​(po​)=∫A​R(po​,pi​)⋅BRDF(pi​,wi​)⋅Li​(pi​,wi​)⋅∣cos(θ)∣dA" where the "R⁡(po,pi)\displaystyle \operatorname{R}(p_o, p_i)R(po​,pi​)" is the diffusion profile, and the wo\displaystyle w_owo​ is deliberately omitted since the diffuse term is irrelevant to the wo\displaystyle w_owo​.

Currently, all real time methods assume a homogeneous material, and the R⁡(po,pi)\displaystyle \operatorname{R}(p_o, p_i)R(po​,pi​) can be considered radially symmetric, namely, R⁡(distance⁡(po,pi))\displaystyle \operatorname{R}(\operatorname{distance}(p_o, p_i))R(distance(po​,pi​)). And the diffuse term can be simplified to "L⁡o(po)=∫AR⁡(distance⁡(po,pi))⋅BRDF⁡(pi,wi)⋅Li⁡(pi,wi)⋅∣cos⁡(θ)∣ dA\displaystyle \operatorname{L}_o(p_o) = \int_A \operatorname{R}(\operatorname{distance}(p_o, p_i)) \cdot \operatorname{BRDF}(p_i, w_i) \cdot \operatorname{L_i}(p_i, w_i) \cdot |\cos(\theta)| \, dALo​(po​)=∫A​R(distance(po​,pi​))⋅BRDF(pi​,wi​)⋅Li​(pi​,wi​)⋅∣cos(θ)∣dA".

However, although the method based on the diffusion profile is much simpler than the BSSRDF, a trivial one pass 2D convolution is still too expensive for real time rendering. Actually, if the width of the efficient domain of the diffusion profile is about 16 mm, and the width of pixel is about 0.25 mm, we would need a 4096(64 x 64) tap blur shader. Thus, a more efficient method needs to be proposed.

1. FaceWorks - NVIDIA

1-1. Subsurface Scattering

The "Subsurface Scattering" of FaceWorks is based on [Penner 2011], and the related source code in FaceWorks is the "EvaluateSSSDiffuseLight" function in "lighting.hlsli".

The main idea of [Penner 2011] is that the "BRDF⁡(pi,wi)\displaystyle \operatorname{BRDF}(p_i, w_i)BRDF(pi​,wi​)" is assumed to be the constant "BRDF⁡(po,wi)\displaystyle \operatorname{BRDF}(p_o, w_i)BRDF(po​,wi​)" for all vicinal locations.

And thus, the "R⁡(distance⁡(po,pi))⋅∣cos⁡(θ)∣\displaystyle \operatorname{R}(\operatorname{distance}(p_o, p_i)) \cdot |\cos(\theta)|R(distance(po​,pi​))⋅∣cos(θ)∣" part of the diffuse term can be "Pre-Integrated" and stored in a LUT(Look Up Texture) which is called the "D⁡(θ,1r)\displaystyle \operatorname{D}(\theta, \frac{1}{r})D(θ,r1​)".

The "θ" is merely the traditional "dot(N,L)", and the "1r\displaystyle \frac{1}{r}r1​", which is called "curvature" by [Penner 2011], can be calculated on-the-fly as "1r=ddx⁡(N)ddx⁡(P)\displaystyle \frac{1}{r} = \frac{\operatorname{ddx}(N)}{\operatorname{ddx}(P)}r1​=ddx(P)ddx(N)​". However, the "on-the-fly" method may be inaccurate since FaceWorks chooses to precompute the curvature instead.

In conclusion, the diffuse term can be calculated as "D⁡(dot⁡(N,L),ddx⁡(N)ddx⁡(P))⋅BRDF⁡(P)⋅LI\displaystyle \operatorname{D}(\operatorname{dot}(N,L), \frac{\operatorname{ddx}(N)}{\operatorname{ddx}(P)}) \cdot \operatorname{BRDF}(P) \cdot LID(dot(N,L),ddx(P)ddx(N)​)⋅BRDF(P)⋅LI".

Usually, the diffusion profile is normalized which indicates the energy conservation. This means that ∫0∞R(r) dr=1\displaystyle \int_0^\infin R(r) \, dr =1∫0∞​R(r)dr=1.

However, according to [Penner 2011], D⁡(θ,1r)=∫−π2π2∣cos⁡(θ+x)∣⋅R(2rsin⁡(x2)) dx∫−π2π2R(2rsin⁡(y2)) dy\displaystyle \operatorname{D}(\theta, \frac{1}{r}) = \frac{\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} | \cos (\theta + x) | \cdot R(2r\sin(\frac{x}{2})) \,dx}{\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} R(2r\sin(\frac{y}{2})) \,dy}D(θ,r1​)=∫−2π​2π​​R(2rsin(2y​))dy∫−2π​2π​​∣cos(θ+x)∣⋅R(2rsin(2x​))dx​. The denominator is added to make sure the diffusion profile is normalized.

Advantages of a Sum-of-Gaussians Diffusion Profile

However, each Gaussian convolution texture can be computed separably and, provided our fit is accurate, the weighted sum of these textures will accurately approximate the lengthy 2D convolution by the original, nonseparable function. This provides a nice way to accelerate general convolutions by nonseparable functions: approximate a nonseparable convolution with a sum of separable convolutions. We use this same idea to accelerate a high-dynamic-range (HDR) bloom filter in Section 14.6.
